Much of what we buy becomes waste. Also, only a small amount of what we recycle is actually recycled.

According to a 2016 report published by Environment and Climate Change Canada, Canadians generated approximately 34 million tonnes of municipal solid waste - of which only 27% was diverted through material recovery facilities (i.e. recycling).

Within residential waste, the largest categories were Food (28%) followed by Plastics (13%), Diapers & Pet Waste (12%), and Paper (10%).

While knowledge about the damaging effects of over-consumption are becoming common knowledge and a mainstream issue within local, national, and international politics we have yet to see a scalable consumer-focused solution to the problem.

There has been a huge movement in recent years toward the circular economy and a focus on the prevention-minimisation-reuse phases of the waste management hierarchy.

However, many of the initiatives are falling short as they attempt to change the macro habitual behavior of users without addressing the micro pain points.

Considering - as the biggest waste categories - food and plastics, and framing this in the consumer message to adopt a zero-waste mindset and move towards reusable containers we can try to identify the barriers to mass-adoption of this norm:

Practicality
Accurately predicting the amount/size of containers needed for each food shop, as well as the act of carrying containers is a serious barrier to adoption for most consumers.

Hygiene Concerns
Following the pandemic, health and safety concerns within the shared economy space are heightened.

Accessibility & Convenience
Younger demographics are becoming more environmentally conscious - enthusiastic adopters of zero-waste initiatives. Allied with less car ownership and a more local mindset for household goods purchases.

Taking into account the problems we are trying to solve, we can attempt to provide solutions which speak directly to each barrier:

Practicality
Remove the burden of providing the refillable container from the consumer by making containers available at the point of purchase.

Hygiene Concerns
In managing the container to address practicality, many of the hygiene concerns can also be mediated by controlling the contaminants entering the system.

Accessibility & Convenience
In the spirit of more open technology, integrations, plugins, and community resources: reduce friction and meet the consumer where they are.

Underpinning these solutions with intuitive software which harnesses nudge theory, gamification, and incentives to subtly encourage positive behavior change in relation to waste consciousness at an individual level would provide a sound business proposition and a net positive environmental impact.

Practicality & Hygiene
Direct competition in the practicality and hygiene space is covered by those working directly with containers (ShareWares, Loop). These apps have more focus on the retailer, which has the potential to make a huge difference. While users still frequent the supermarket they will continue to purchase more than they need.

Accessibility & Conveniences
Additional competition can be found within the food delivery marketplace, including groceries (Tiggy, Instacart), takeaway food (Uber Eats, SkipTheDishes), and meal planning (Fresh Prep, Hello Fresh). All directly influence accessibility and potentially help consumers buy less, waste less - but these solutions have also been criticized for - in some cases - an increase in packaging waste.

Behavioral Change
Broadly these can be categorized into apps which direct the user to food which is about to be wasted (Flash Food, Foodful.ly, Too Good To Go) and those which direct the user to zero-waste stores/infrastructure (Quench, Bulk Locator). Apps in this space provide excellent resources without a heavy focus on affecting habitual change.

To investigate the similarities in musicality across each playlist, I employed the statistical analysis technique k-means clustering to group each track from the playlist based on sonic qualities as provided by the Spotify Web API.

Although not statistically significant, the results suggest my curated playlist [shoegaze] contained only three clusters, whereas the Spotify playlists demonstrated six different sonic clusters - the tracks included show much more variation in musicality.

Further investigation of sonic qualities across the three playlists shows the consistency of sound across my curated playlist more clearly. Although further investigation is required, the data suggests that a popular artist on Spotify will engage a wide variety of users and will be played/playlisted in many different circumstances.

A popular artist with a varied back catalogue - like The Beatles - might be included in a playlist of ballads as well as one of psychedelic rock and these very different actions can result in recommendations with significant sonic variation.

It should be noted that the desire for a consistent sound within a playlist is not a universal requirement.

In order to offer a recommendation on these findings I began with a user profile which was built around Spotify's strategic objective to engage more older users. Only 11% of Spotify users are aged 45-54 year old which means there is less user data and therefore less relevant recommendations. By engaging an initial cohort of older users, the product begins to benefit from a network effect whereby preferences are revealed through actions.

During ideation I engaged in divergent thinking in order to explore solutions including incentivising crowdsourced musicality ratings, a viral engagement tool to encourage users to drag and drop loops from their favourite tracks and build something new [thus revealing underlying musical preferences], and the ability to scan a physical record collection to align Spotify profile with the analogue world.

After considering the pros and cons of various features, weighing up estimated effort against potential customer delight - while maintaining consistency of experience for core users - I proposed the implementation of optionally tweaking the recommendation engine while engaged in a playlist.

A filter tab is added to the bottom of the application, which contains three dials representing the weighting of each characteristic to feed into the recommendation algorithm - play me more tracks that sound like this and less artists closely associated with this track. The track queue will update in real time as the user sets preferences.
