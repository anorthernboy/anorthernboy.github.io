Overview

Much of what we buy becomes waste. Also, only a small amount of what we recycle is actually recycled. According to a 2016 report published by Environment and Climate Change Canada, Canadians generated approximately 34 million tonnes of municipal solid waste - of which only 27% was diverted through material recovery facilities (i.e. recycling). Within residential waste, the largest categories were Food (28%) followed by Plastics (13%), Diapers & Pet Waste (12%), and Paper (10%).

Problem

While knowledge about the damaging effects of over-consumption are becoming common knowledge and a mainstream issue within local, national, and international politics we have yet to see a scalable consumer-focused solution to the problem. There has been a huge movement in recent years toward the circular economy and a focus on the prevention-minimisation-reuse phases of the waste management hierarchy. However, many of the initiatives are falling short as they attempt to change the macro habitual behavior of users without addressing the micro pain points.

Considering - as the biggest waste categories - food and plastics, and framing this in the consumer message to adopt a zero-waste mindset and move towards reusable containers we can try to identify the barriers to mass-adoption of this norm:

Practicality
Accurately predicting the amount/size of containers needed for each food shop, as well as the act of carrying containers is a serious barrier to adoption for most consumers.

Hygiene Concerns
Following the pandemic, health and safety concerns within the shared economy space are heightened.

Accessibility & Convenience
Younger demographics are becoming more environmentally conscious - enthusiastic adopters of zero-waste initiatives. Allied with less car ownership and a more local mindset for household goods purchases.

Solution

Taking into account the problems we are trying to solve, we can attempt to provide solutions which speak directly to each barrier. Underpinning these solutions with intuitive software which harnesses nudge theory, gamification, and incentives to subtly encourage positive behavior change in relation to waste consciousness at an individual level would provide a sound business proposition and a net positive environmental impact.

Practicality
Remove the burden of providing the refillable container from the consumer by making containers available at the point of purchase.

Hygiene Concerns
In managing the container to address practicality, many of the hygiene concerns can also be mediated by controlling the contaminants entering the system.

Accessibility & Convenience
In the spirit of more open technology, integrations, plugins, and community resources: reduce friction and meet the consumer where they are.

Competition

Practicality & Hygiene
Direct competition in the practicality and hygiene space is covered by those working directly with containers (ShareWares, Loop). These apps have more focus on the retailer, which has the potential to make a huge difference. While users still frequent the supermarket they will continue to purchase more than they need.

Accessibility & Conveniences
Additional competition can be found within the food delivery marketplace, including groceries (Tiggy, Instacart), takeaway food (Uber Eats, SkipTheDishes), and meal planning (Fresh Prep, Hello Fresh). All directly influence accessibility and potentially help consumers buy less, waste less - but these solutions have also been criticized for - in some cases - an increase in packaging waste.

Behavioral Change
Broadly these can be categorized into apps which direct the user to food which is about to be wasted (Flash Food, Foodful.ly, Too Good To Go) and those which direct the user to zero-waste stores/infrastructure (Quench, Bulk Locator). Apps in this space provide excellent resources without a heavy focus on affecting habitual change.

Product Vision

For conscious consumers who want to reduce household waste Pantrii is a technology powered store cupboard that empowers consumers to buy less, waste less. Unlike traditional zero-waste stores Pantrii is available in hundreds of residential developments in major cities across North America.

Bio
Oliver has a full-time job as a Social Media Manager at a progressive digital agency. He is passionate about living efficiently and enjoys exploring hands-on projects outside of work such as brewing beer and wine at home. He cares about the environment and feels guilty about the amount of waste he produces. Additionally, he strongly dislikes visiting the communal waste room so often. Oliver has started to explore the zero-waste movement, following initiatives on social media channels but feels frustrated he doesn’t have access to a zero-waste store within walking distance of his home. Oliver often picks up groceries on the cycle home from work, it is unrealistic for Oliver to carry enough empty containers for his usual shopping habits. Living in a small apartment in a big city, Oliver is interested in utilizing the shared economy in order to reduce his possessions. Oliver is very tech savvy, and is excited to try out new technology.

Ideation & Sketching

Feature Prioritization & User Flows

Further project depth would allow deeper exploration of feature requirements, including - ideally - prioritization via a decision matrix plotting feasibility against desirability. In lieu of this, some key features supporting the value proposition are presented in the following user flows.

Onboarding

The onboarding flow offers signup via email of social media, with profile creation following. The user is presented with a map of Pantrii locations and asked to select a home Pantrii. Depending on revenue models there could possibly be an additional option to add multiple Pantrii’s to the account as a premium feature. Users are given the option to add another user to the account and an onboarding prompt is designed to personalize or tailor content for the user.

Purchase

Using the phone to access the Pantrii, the user receives a notification and can head straight to the menu. Adding an item is the primary CTA and there is an animated interaction on increasing the amount with the slider. When checking out, all items are dispensed from the vending machine type dispensary. Alternatively, the user might prepare an order before visiting the Pantrii and a notification on arrival asks the user if they would like to dispense.

Manage

In the interest of reducing waste, this scenario presents a smart notification prompting the user to review their previous purchases. In this flow, we are assuming Pantrii has produced smart IOT containers and is aware that the rigatoni has not yet been used. The user is taken to their Pantrii, to see all their items stored in their household. Within each item is the option to send to the marketplace (share with another user) or explore some recipe ideas.

Future Pantrii developments could move beyond store cupboard staples and provide all ingredients for a recommended recipe. Collecting behavioral data on users interacting with a given Pantrii could unlock opportunities to manage food waste across all categories.

Progress

In order to affect habitual change in users the progress dashboard contains milestones, achievements, badges, rankings, etc. Elements of gamification would be developed in this area of the user profile and potentially incentives or tokens which could hold some monetary value within the zero-waste ecosystem.
