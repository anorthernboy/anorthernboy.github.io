Avalanche Insights data exploration platform design

A zero-to-one design project to enable a shift from a service-based to SaaS-based revenue model

Overview

Avalanche Insights is a successful service-based consultancy in the progressive politics space, focused on extracting key themes and insights from open-ended text data. In order to scale their services, Avalanche Insights needed to productize their process. Developing a robust process for analyzing open-ended text data was crucial to creating a scalable product.


Goals

- Build a platform to display themes and insights
- Build a platform which allows a general user to intuitively explore themes and insights


Role

- User Researcher
- UI/UX Designer


Tools

- Lucid (survey platform)
- Respondent (cohort recruitment platform)
- Zoom
- MIRO
- Confluence
- Figma


Research and Discovery

To better understand the market and potential users, a competitive analysis was conducted to understand design language and to find potential users through product testimonials on similar products. A survey was then conducted to build a demographic picture of potential users who react to LinkedIn ads offering a solution to analyzing open-ended text data.


The survey results identified three potential user groups: human resources managers conducting workplace surveys, marketing teams gauging new product launches, and product managers or user researchers gathering product feedback. User interviews were conducted to further understand the jobs-to-be-done and related pain points users experience within their existing processes. Based on the results, user personas were built out in more depth and user journeys were established.


Initial sketches and product concepts were then created and informed further rounds of discovery interviews, gathering feedback, testing hypotheses, and updating personas and journey maps. A jobs-to-be-done framework was established based on the user interviews. Continual exploration of solutions to user problems was done through ongoing discovery interviews.

Gains

Journey Map

Extensive discovery interviews allowed me to develop a journey map with a number of stages that were constantly discussed by users. Initially (collect) the users were excited to start a new product, and reported a general contentment with their survey fielding software.

The very next stage (analyse) provided the biggest frustration, this was the realisation - often described as overwhelming - that the user would be required to sift through all survey responses to tease out themes and insights. It was typically reported that, even though the survey fielding tools included features to address this stage, their functionality was insufficient for the user requirements.

The third stage (curate) included pulling together visualisations, key quotes, and highlights to create presentation decks to report back to the organisation. Although this didn't cause the same dread as the previous stage, there were still some challenges around exploring data visualisations and crafting relevant stories.

The final stage for the user (share) is the final handoff of the recommendations and findings. At this stage the user reported feeling relieved having come through the previous two stages.


Design and Prototyping

To satisfy user needs, the design process involved moving from ideation to testing with interactive prototypes. Feedback loops informed user personas and journey maps throughout the process. The fidelity was gradually increased from sketches to wireframes, higher fidelity screens, and finally interactive prototypes. Feedback was sought from potential users through further interviews and usability tests.


The discovery and design process outlined above was repeated continually to explore solutions to user problems identified and prioritised through ongoing discovery interviews.

In continuing the test-validate-iterate cycle employed in the discovery phase I was able to improve both the utility and efficiency of my design process and increase the likelihood of creating value for the end user.


Screens

In the spirit of lean methodology, I chose to use an open source design system which included excellent documentation and best practice design patterns (in addition to the modular design components). Using an open source design system saved a huge amount of time and effort at all stages of the product development lifecycle. It ensured the design was consistent across pages, allowed me to focus on solving new or novel problems, and made the handoff process much more fluid.

Key features supporting the value proposition are presented in the following user flows:

Upload

The purpose of our Beta platform was to showcase the proprietary data science we were building. As such, we were not initially addressing the stage of the user journey which covered designing and fielding a survey - that would continue to happen on other platforms.

This presented a somewhat challenging user experience problem. Although we were accepting data uploads from any survey platform - with their many small differences in data export structure - we were not going to invest heavily in engineering a solution (given limited resources).

Through a combination of user interviews and technical requirements I was able to generate a solution which offered as little friction as possible for the user while ensuring the uploaded data was accurately stored in our database schema.



Start Analysis

The human-in-the-loop thematic analysis functionality addresses the major pain point outlined in the journey map - analysing large amounts of open-ended text data to derive themes, insights, and - ultimately - value. After the user has completed their upload to the platform, they can access the analysis dashboard in an empty state. There is a call to action, leading the user to begin the machine assisted analysis process.

Cluster analysis is performed on the upload and the user is able to start their analysis with some smart themes which are fully customisable. This feature was a key value proposition discovered through user interviews where users would describe feeling overwhelmed starting with a blank slate and would like a headstart on their analysis.

As this feature forms such a large part of the product - the key value proposition - the design process is covered in detail here.



Dashboard

A dashboard is a key element in most SaaS platforms, and Avalanche Insights was no exception. The output of the service-based consultancy work at Avalanche Insights was a deck which contained many static charts, which had been manually built in a BI tool. The successes of this product formed the basis of the SaaS dashboard.

Building from the Avalanche Insights consultancy deck - but crucially adding interaction for customer data exploration - I also performed an in depth review of other data visualisation platforms and their key features.

With the developing user personas as reference, I created and tested many iterations of the dashboard and components.

Share

During discovery interviews, one of the most consistent concepts discussed by users was the idea that a survey isn’t just conducted for its own sake - almost always there is a requirement to report findings to one or many different stakeholders at the end of the project.

Sharing functionality within the platform allows users to centralise the findings, and create bespoke report pages for different stakeholders - inviting the stakeholder to the platform or sending a report via email. Crucially, the data can be curated for each audience - ensuring the language used to express, for example, a theme, is consistent with the language used within the company culture.



Admin

Admin settings are key infrastructure for any SaaS platform. Primarily in the management of users and data. During the early discovery phase of user interviews, there were a lot of conversations around personal data, and data collection for the sake of data collection. This was amplified somewhat by the fact that the user already had data stored on a survey platform, and was required to bring it to the Avalanche Insights platform.

In order to remedy some major user concerns, we developed robust access controls and I created a simple and clean admin menu for the management of permissions.



Outcome

The prototypes and MVP were used extensively in investor pitches resulting in a successful seed round led by a top VC. Avalanche Insights secured their first SaaS customers, including a global streaming platform. They were able to deliver recurring revenue to the value of $20k USD per month, enabling a pivot to a SaaS-based revenue model. A Beta platform was launched within six months, supported by a magic box experience in the backend due to the proprietary data science not being sufficiently productized.

Lessons Learned

The project encountered challenges and ambiguities, highlighting the importance of continual research and discovery to establish customer needs and jobs-to-be-done. The user personas and journey maps served as reference points throughout the design process. Gradually increasing fidelity from sketches to interactive prototypes ensured user satisfaction. Feedback from potential users was sought and incorporated throughout the process.

Conclusion

The key points and lessons learned from the project were summarized, highlighting the importance of research, design, and prototyping. The implications for future product design projects were discussed, emphasizing the need for continual research and feedback throughout the design process. Overall, the project demonstrated the importance of a thorough design process to successfully create a scalable product.
