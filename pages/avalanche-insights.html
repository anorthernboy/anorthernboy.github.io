Avalanche Insights data exploration platform design

A zero-to-one design project to enable a shift from a service-based to SaaS-based revenue model

Overview

Avalanche Insights is a successful service-based consultancy in the progressive politics space, focused on extracting key themes and insights from open-ended text data. In order to scale their services, Avalanche Insights needed to productize their process. Developing a robust process for analysing open-ended text data was crucial to creating a scalable product.


Goals

- Build a platform to display themes and insights
- Build a platform which allows a general user to intuitively explore themes and insights


Role

- User Researcher
- UI/UX Designer


Tools

- Lucid (survey platform)
- Respondent (cohort recruitment platform)
- Zoom
- MIRO
- Confluence
- Figma


Research and Discovery

To better understand the market and potential users, a competitive analysis was conducted to understand design language and to find potential users through product testimonials on similar products. A survey was then conducted to build a demographic picture of potential users who react to LinkedIn ads offering a solution to analyzing open-ended text data.

The survey results identified four potential user groups: human resources managers, customer experience managers, brand managers, and market researchers.


Human resources managers were the biggest group of respondents and were also 90% more likely than all other respondents to rely on 'Manually reading through each response' when analysing open-ended data. Respondents working in human resources identified several benefits of using a product that combines AI and human judgment to deliver actionable insights from employee feedback.

Firstly, such a product can provide fast, nuanced, and customised insights that can help users make informed decisions. This is particularly important for human resources managers who require accurate and reliable insights from employee feedback to make informed decisions. Secondly, using AI can help streamline the process of analysing open-ended feedback and extracting meaningful insights, which can save time and resources for users. Thirdly, the product can help users gain a deeper understanding of their employees' needs and concerns, which can help improve the employee experience and workplace culture. Finally, users value a combination of automated tools and human judgment, as this approach can ensure the insights generated are both reliable and relevant to their organisation.


Respondents working in human resources responded overwhelmingly positively to the question 'How likely are you to use a product that combines AI and human judgment to deliver actionable insights from customer, brand, and employee feedback?' This was an interesting finding given that only 14% of human resources respondents reported using automated text processing tools in their current workflow.

Respondents raised concerns about the integration of the product with existing processes and systems, its cost-effectiveness, and the reliability and relevance of the insights generated. They emphasised the importance of maintaining a human touch in the analysis process and ensuring that the insights generated were accurate, reliable, and relevant to their organisations.

Human resources managers, in particular, expressed concern about the potential ethical implications of using AI in analysing sensitive employee data, the accuracy and reliability of the insights generated, and the potential impact on employee privacy and trust. They emphasised the need for transparency and accountability in the analysis process and the importance of maintaining trust with employees throughout the process.

Surfacing these crucial concerns at such an early stage in the process allowed the team to build this into the value proposition and product requirements.


The next stage of discovery required more depth, which meant user interviews. To begin the process, I identified potential users and reached out to them to schedule interviews. I prepared a list of open-ended questions that would help me understand their experience and perspective related to the product. During the interviews, I focused on active listening and creating a comfortable environment for users to share their thoughts and opinions. I used a mix of probing questions and follow-up questions to dig deeper into specific topics and clarify any ambiguities.


After conducting several interviews, I analysed the data and identified patterns and themes that emerged. I used this information to create user personas that represented the different types of users and their goals, needs, and pain points. The perspective of these personas were then used to inform initial ideas on the user journey, lifting the common themes from discovery interviews and arranging them in a recurring step-by-step process.

The discovery interview process was instrumental in helping me understand the user's perspective and design a product that met their needs. By using a combination of active listening, probing questions, and user personas, I was able to gather valuable insights that informed the entire design process.


Initial sketches and product concepts were then created and informed further rounds of discovery interviews, gathering feedback, testing hypotheses, and updating personas and journey maps. A jobs-to-be-done framework was established based on the user interviews. Continual exploration of solutions to user problems was done through ongoing discovery interviews.

Journey Map

Extensive discovery interviews allowed me to develop a journey map with a number of stages that were constantly discussed by users. Initially (collect) the users were excited to start a new product, and reported a general contentment with their survey fielding software.

The very next stage (analyse) provided the biggest frustration, this was the realisation - often described as overwhelming - that the user would be required to sift through all survey responses to tease out themes and insights. It was typically reported that, even though the survey fielding tools included features to address this stage, their functionality was insufficient for the user requirements.

The third stage (curate) included pulling together visualisations, key quotes, and highlights to create presentation decks to report back to the organisation. Although this didn't cause the same dread as the previous stage, there were still some challenges around exploring data visualisations and crafting relevant stories.

The final stage for the user (share) is the final handoff of the recommendations and findings. At this stage the user reported feeling relieved having come through the previous two stages.


Information Architecture & Sitemap

At this stage I was able to outline an initial draft of the information architecture (IA) and sitemap. To ensure the IA and sitemap aligned with the product strategy and technical constraints, I collaborated closely with the product management and engineering teams throughout the process.

First, I gathered information about the application and created an inventory of all pages and elements. Next, I worked with the product management team to develop a user flow and organize the content into a logical structure. I then created a sitemap to visualize the hierarchy of pages and content, which was shared with the product management and engineering teams for feedback.

Finally, I tested the IA and sitemap with users and worked with the product management and engineering teams to refine the structure based on their feedback. By involving all teams throughout the design process, we were able to create an effective IA and sitemap that met the needs of our users while also aligning with the overall product strategy and technical constraints.


Design and Prototyping

To satisfy user needs, the design process involved moving from ideation to testing with interactive prototypes. Feedback loops informed user personas and journey maps throughout the process. The fidelity was gradually increased from sketches to wireframes, higher fidelity screens, and finally interactive prototypes. Feedback was sought from potential users through further interviews and usability tests.


The discovery and design process outlined above was repeated continually to explore solutions to user problems identified and prioritised through ongoing discovery interviews.

In continuing the test-validate-iterate cycle employed in the discovery phase I was able to improve both the utility and efficiency of my design process and increase the likelihood of creating value for the end user.


Screens

In the spirit of lean methodology, I chose to use an open source design system which included excellent documentation and best practice design patterns (in addition to the modular design components). Using an open source design system saved a huge amount of time and effort at all stages of the product development lifecycle. It ensured the design was consistent across pages, allowed me to focus on solving new or novel problems, and made the handoff process much more fluid.

Key features supporting the value proposition are presented in the following user flows:

Upload

Our Beta platform aimed to showcase our proprietary data science capabilities, but it did not cover the stage of the user journey that involved designing and fielding a survey, which would still take place on other platforms. This created a challenge in terms of user experience. Although our platform could accept data uploads from any survey platform, each with its own small differences in data export structure, we did not want to invest heavily in engineering a solution due to limited resources.

To address this challenge, I conducted user interviews and analysed technical requirements to generate a solution that minimised user friction while ensuring accurate storage of uploaded data in our database schema. The solution was designed to offer a seamless experience for the user, and involved a combination of technical adjustments and user-friendly features. By leveraging insights from user interviews and technical requirements, we were able to develop a solution that successfully addressed this user experience challenge.



Start Analysis

Our platform's thematic analysis functionality was designed to address the key challenge identified in our user journey map - the difficulty of analysing large amounts of open-ended text data to extract themes and insights. Once the user uploads their data to the platform, they are directed to an empty state on the analysis dashboard. There, they can initiate the machine-assisted analysis process by following a prominent call-to-action.

Our platform uses cluster analysis to generate intelligent themes that the user can customise to their liking. This feature was a crucial value proposition that we discovered through user interviews. Our research showed that users often felt overwhelmed when starting with a blank slate and desired a head start on their analysis. The smart themes feature offers users a jumpstart on their analysis while still allowing for customisation.

Overall, the human-in-the-loop thematic analysis functionality is a key value proposition of our platform, as it offers users an efficient and intuitive way to analyse their text data and extract valuable insights.

As this feature forms such a large part of the product the design process is covered in detail here.



Dashboard

Avalanche Insights' SaaS platform required a dashboard to present its data visualisations and insights. To create this dashboard, I drew inspiration from the static charts of the consultancy deck that Avalanche Insights produced using various BI tools. Crucially, I added interactive features to enable customer data exploration. To ensure that the resulting dashboard was competitive, I conducted an extensive analysis of other data visualisation platforms and their key features.

Using the evolving user personas as a guide, I designed and tested multiple iterations of the dashboard and its components. The goal was to create a dashboard that provided a streamlined user experience, with easy-to-understand visualisations and clear calls to action. The Avalanche Insights SaaS dashboard was designed to provide users with a powerful tool for exploring data and uncovering insights.



Group By

The ability to segment data is a fundamental feature in any data analysis tool, and was identified as a key requirement through user interviews. Users often begin their analysis by segmenting data based on key demographic variables such as age or gender, in order to gain a better understanding of how themes and insights differ across different groups.

To address this need, I implemented a basic segmentation feature in the platform. This feature allows users to quickly toggle between different demographic questions and instantly view the distribution of themes across basic segments. By providing this functionality, we aimed to reduce the time and effort required for users to conduct basic segmentation, and to enable them to quickly gain insights from their data. This feature was tested extensively through user testing and feedback was incorporated into several iterations to refine the functionality and improve the user experience.





Filter

When conducting user research, it became clear that some advanced users needed more robust segmentation capabilities than the basic demographic questions offered. To address this need, I designed and tested an interaction that allows users to combine multiple attributes to create custom segments.

Drawing on common patterns found in enterprise products, the resulting interface was intuitive and easy to use. During testing, users looking for additional segmentation functionality were able to discover and use this feature quickly and without confusion.

This new capability proved to be a valuable addition to the platform, providing more advanced users with the flexibility and control they needed to gain deeper insights from their data.

Share

The discovery interviews revealed a consistent requirement for users to report survey findings to different stakeholders at the end of a project. To address this, the platform includes sharing functionality that allows users to create customised report pages for each stakeholder. Users can invite stakeholders to the platform or send reports via email.

This feature is particularly valuable because it enables users to curate the data for each audience, ensuring consistency with the language used within the company culture. This was identified as a critical requirement during user interviews and was iteratively tested to ensure it met users' needs.



Admin

In order to address user concerns around personal data and data collection during the early discovery phase, robust access controls were developed for the admin settings of the Avalanche Insights platform. These controls enabled the simple and clean management of permissions, ensuring that user data was only accessible by those with the appropriate level of authorisation.

Through the development of these controls, we were able to remedy major user concerns around data security and privacy. Additionally, the admin menu was designed to be intuitive and user-friendly, allowing users to easily manage permissions and access controls without requiring extensive technical knowledge or training. Overall, the robust access controls and simple admin menu were crucial elements in ensuring user trust and satisfaction with the Avalanche Insights platform.



Outcome

The prototypes and MVP were used extensively in investor pitches resulting in a successful seed round led by a top VC. Avalanche Insights secured their first SaaS customers, including a global streaming platform. They were able to deliver recurring revenue to the value of $20k USD per month, enabling a pivot to a SaaS-based revenue model. A Beta platform was launched within six months, supported by a magic box experience in the backend due to the proprietary data science not being sufficiently productized.

Lessons Learned

The project encountered challenges and ambiguities, highlighting the importance of continual research and discovery to establish customer needs and jobs-to-be-done. The user personas and journey maps served as reference points throughout the design process. Gradually increasing fidelity from sketches to interactive prototypes ensured user satisfaction. Feedback from potential users was sought and incorporated throughout the process.

Conclusion

The key points and lessons learned from the project were summarised, highlighting the importance of research, design, and prototyping. The implications for future product design projects were discussed, emphasising the need for continual research and feedback throughout the design process. Overall, the project demonstrated the importance of a thorough design process to successfully create a scalable product.
